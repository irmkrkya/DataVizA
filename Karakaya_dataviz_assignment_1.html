First families move into new homes in Bosnia two decades after the war

The first returnee internally displaced families in Bosnia and Herzegovina moved into their newly reconstructed houses in April of 2016 under a housing program.

The Regional Housing Program has been providing housing to people who fled Croatia and Bosnia and Herzegovina during the 1990 Balkan wars.

According to the UNHCR report, a total of 101 million euros from the Regional Housing Program has been committed to Bosnia and Herzegovina. Funding has been used to build homes for 5, 400 returnees, refugees and internally displaced people in Bosnia and Herzegovina. As part of the project, Serbia laid the foundation stone for the construction of the 235 apartments in last September. The project's Serbian part is expected to resolve the housing problems of more than 45,000 people who are living in Serbia.

During the conflicts of the 1990s, around 2.2 million people from Bosnia was displaced or become refugees. The number of displaced people was believed to be around 550,000 in Croatia and 540,000 in Serbia.

Karadzic guilty verdict stirs mixed reaction from Bosniaks and Serbs

Former Bosnian Serb leader Radovan Karadzic has been convicted of genocide and war crimes in the Bosnian war, and sentenced to 40 years in jail in last March. His conviction caused mixed reactions among Bosniaks and Serbs. While some people welcomed the court rule, others emphasized that I will never be enough to heal the wounds that the war created in both sides.

“It doesn't matter what the verdict is at this stage as it won't give any inner peace to people here. Too much happened and I don't think people will ever move on properly,” Asim Delalic, 25 year-old Bosniak from Sarajevo said.

judges in The Hague hold Karadžić responsible for his role in lethal ethnic cleansing operations, the siege of Sarajevo and the slaughter of 8,000 Muslim men and boys in Srebrenica in 1995.

Edna Siljdedic, 20 year-old Bosniak woman, and her family moved to San Francisco as refugees after the war. She expressed excitement about the verdict.“It's easy to be a pessimist, but today's conviction of Radovan Karadzic gives me hope. Despite war and genocide, we continue to stay strong and to live,” Siljdedic said.

Daniel Milosevic, 34 year-old Bosnian Serb, lives in Ohio, said the verdict is step backwards for the reconciliation of the nation and contributes the feeling that Serbs have about their side of story was never told. “This verdict won't change anything. I think that wounds will never heal back home,” he noted.

Access to basic rights for Bosnian and Croatian refugees still obstacle in Serbia

Despite of protracted refugee situation, access to basic rights such as free health public services in Serbia could be not possible for the country’s refugees.

“Vulnerable refugees often live in inadequate living conditions, either in substandard private accommodation or collective centers, in remote areas, with insufficient means to provide enough means for basic needs, medical treatments and without prospects to obtain adequate and durable housing solutions on their own,” according to UNHRC.

UNHCR says its office in Serbia has been advocating for the closure of refugee chapter for refugees from Croatia and Bosnia and Herzegovina and promotes the Regional Housing Project for durable solution.

More than of 35 thousand Croatian and Bosnian refugees still displaced in Serbia

More than 35 thousand refugees from Croatia and Bosnia are still displaced in Serbia, according to Danish Refugee Council.

There were nine collective centers in the country as of January of last year and these centers were hosting 185 Refugees from Croatia and Bosnia and 375 Internally Displaced Persons from Kosovo the council reported. According to Danish Refugee Council, refugees have been often living in poor conditions and without an adequate alternative housing solution.

“The number of refugees continues to reduce as a result of the local integration and return projects, as well as the Regional Housing Programme. Even though the process of voluntary repatriation has not yet been completed, it is assumed that the majority of refugees have opted for local integration,” Danish Refugee Council, says.
